Emissions Estimation Overview


Data Explanation

Using the Emissions Estimation Tool, you will see your zip code’s estimated per capita greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions data displayed in the form of a heat map, pie chart, and bar chart showing GHG emissions by sources. The data for your county will be displayed by zip code in units of mT CO2e/year, broken down into four major sectors of your emissions.


What is CO2e and why should I care about it?

CO2e is the standardized measurement of greenhouse gasses. It allows us to express a carbon footprint comprised of many greenhouse gasses with one equivalent unit. Carbon Dioxide (CO2), Methane (NH4), and Nitrous Oxide (N2O) are all greenhouse gasses, however they each have a different global warming potential (GWP) once released into the atmosphere. Global warming potential is measured in relation to Carbon Dioxide, therefore multiplying a chemical’s GWP by the volume (metric tons) released will provide us with the carbon dioxide equivalent volume. Tracking our GHG emissions enables us to reduce them over time, lessening human contribution to global warming

1 mT CO2e is equal to:

2513 miles driven with an average passenger vehicle

113 gallons of gasoline consumed

66.4 homes electricity use for a day

121,643 smartphones charged

42.5 trash bags of waste recycled instead of landfilled

1.75 wind turbines running for an hour

A Discussion of GHG Emissions Scope

Original image Source: Global Protocol for Community-Scale Greenhouse Gas Emission Inventories: https://ghgprotocol.org/sites/default/files/standards_supporting/GPC_Executive_Summary_1.pdf


For the purposes of this estimation, we chose to focus on mostly “in-boundary” GHG emissions, or those generated within the zip code, city or county boundary of interest. Therefore, source GHG emissions include the following for any given municipality within Colorado: emissions from electricity distributed per-person and per-business, average emissions from natural gas combustion per person and per business, emissions from all passenger and freight vehicle miles travelled, emissions from any landfills within the municipality, and emissions from any cement or manufacturing facilities within the municipality. The only “out of boundary” emissions we included is an estimation of GHG emissions from airline transportation, based on a national per-person average. This scope was chosen based on public availability of data as well as areas where local governance has influence.

General Methodology

To start off, the team researched publicly available data for the main sectors of emissions, Energy, Vehicles and Transportation, and Waste and Management. With the data for each major sector and its subsectors providing different data types, the team worked together to convert into CO2e (Carbon Dioxide Equivalent). Since the publicly available data consisted of varying data values in different units, the team started the calculation in excel before exporting the data into the SQL database. Limitations of our In-boundary Emissions Estimation

Ultimately, our per-capita estimation is less a representation of the behavior of residents and is instead a snapshot of the volume of waste collected and fossil fuels combusted for activities occurring within the municipality boundaries. This is beneficial information for regional administration to consider when identifying actions to reduce emissions from sources within their jurisdiction.


  • Zip Codes with a population of zero have an “infinity” GHG emissions per capita. These are likely industrial facilities or business parks.
  • Similarly, areas with very low populations show a very high per capita emissions if there are high emissions business(es) within the boundary
  • High tourism areas portray a very high per capita GHG emissions regardless of the behaviors of residents
  • Transportation – Given that this represents in-boundary vehicle miles traveled (VMT), a percentage of emissions is generated by through traffic and thus is outside of the locality’s scope of management
  • Construction and Manufacturing – source emissions are allocated to the jurisdiction they reside within and doesn’t portray where the products are consumed.
  • Waste – More populated areas often send their waste to landfills outside of their jurisdiction, thus emissions are not necessarily allocated to the municipalities where they are generated

The process of developing this emissions estimation was to see how accurate of a calculation was possible with publicly accessible data. Our research showed that due to data privacy concerns more localized data (specifically for electricity and natural gas consumption) was unavailable and therefore source emissions are averaged across a larger region.
